FinOps Maturity Assessment Engine — Scanner Architecture & Chain-of-Thinking

The FinOps Engine is not a chatbot with instructions. It is a structured analytical pipeline where each phase feeds the next, and no phase can be skipped. The architecture enforces rigor at every step — the AI extracts evidence, mathematics validates it, and only then does strategy get synthesized.

01How the Engine Thinks

The Forensic Analysis Pipeline

Four primary phases plus four post-strategy verification layers. Each one is constrained by what came before. The chain-of-thinking is deliberate: AI cannot decide alone, mathematics cannot strategize, and strategy cannot rewrite the evidence. Every claim is fact-checked, every survivor is surfaced, every gap is named.

Why This Order Matters

The chain-of-thinking is deliberately constrained:

01

Phase 1 cannot see Phase 3's job.

The forensic auditor only extracts evidence — it does not recommend. This prevents confirmation bias where an AI might score evidence higher because it already has a strategy in mind.

02

Phase 2 has no AI.

The mathematical validation layer is a firewall against hallucination. If Phase 1 scores an item at 2 but the sub-criteria don't support it, Phase 2's math catches the inconsistency.

03

Phase 3 cannot override Phase 2.

The strategy synthesizer receives validated metrics as absolute truth. It cannot re-score items or reinterpret evidence. It can only prescribe actions based on what was mathematically confirmed.

04

The fact-check is independent.

A second LLM call reads the strategy and asks "can this claim be traced?" — the same model that wrote the strategy never grades its own work. Survivors after the regenerate loop are surfaced in the report, not hidden.

This is the critical difference from enterprise search tools like Ayfie, RFP platforms like Responsive and Loopio, AI-native response tools like Arphie — and from generic AI chat: no single AI call controls the entire outcome. The pipeline creates accountability between phases and surfaces its own failures in the artifact it ships.

02
FinOps Forensic Auditor
Phase 1 — Evidence Extraction
Role
A forensic investigator who treats every document as an untrusted source. Does not give benefit of the doubt. Extracts only what can be traced back to specific text in the source material.
Per-Batch Behaviour
Receives 10 criteria definitions (5 maturity + 5 anti-pattern). Each criterion has 3 specific sub-criteria to check. For each criterion, writes a "reasoning" field — a scratchpad proving the connection to source text. Assigns a score of 0–3 based on how many sub-criteria have traceable evidence. Returns structured JSON — not prose, not opinions.
Model · Concurrency
Gemini Flash, optimised for fast parallel extraction. 5 batches run simultaneously. Total Phase 1 duration: ~30–60 seconds.

Behavioural Rules

  • You are a FinOps Forensic Auditor. Your job is to extract explicit proof of cloud financial management practices from the text.
  • CRITICAL: You must NOT give the benefit of the doubt. You are looking for Traceable Evidence.
  • Plans are not evidence. Intent scores 1 at most. Only documented mechanisms, processes, and measurable outcomes score 2 or 3.
  • Tool Presence ≠ Practice. Mentioning a tool does not prove active use.
  • Silence is Data: If a criterion has zero evidence in the documents, score it 0 and note the absence.
  • Every captured evidence quote MUST be tagged with one of seven categories — Policy / Process / Operational / Automation / Accountability / Financial-Integration / Cultural — so the report can answer "do we have process evidence but no automation evidence?"
  • Document-type signatures: a narrow document (e.g., a tagging policy) is EXPECTED to be silent on most batches. Do not invent evidence to "balance" the audit — a score of 0 on an unaddressed batch is the correct answer.
03
FinOps Transformation Architect
Phase 3 — Strategy Synthesis
Role
Not a consultant who hedges. A transformation leader who prescribes specific mechanisms based on validated evidence — and prescribes them three times, in three vocabularies, for three audiences.
What It Receives
(1) Phase 2 validated metrics — treated as absolute truth. (2) Original documents — for proper nouns and organisational context only. (3) FinOps Tactics Database — proven remediation mechanisms with real case studies, each carrying a verbatim TAC-XXX-NNN identifier. (4) Crawl-Walk-Run methodology framework. (5) Three persona definitions (FinOps Lead, CFO, Engineering Lead) with focus areas, language style, and key questions.
What It Produces
Three persona-tailored executive summaries in a single call (same diagnostic facts, three lenses — FinOps Lead vocabulary, CFO ROI/risk framing, Engineering Lead architecture/performance framing). Visual scorecard (headline, maturity score, anti-pattern burden). 4-phase remediation roadmap; every actionable item must cite at least one [TAC-XXX-NNN] ID verbatim from the database. A post-call fact-check pass independently verifies every concrete claim — survivors after up to 2 regenerate retries are surfaced inline as "Confidence Notes" under the affected persona summary.
Model
Gemini Pro — reasoning model, slower but deeper synthesis.

Behavioural Rules (STRATEGY_GUARDRAILS)

  • You are NOT a consultant paid by the hour. You are a FinOps transformation leader who has turned around cloud cost management at scale.
  • Source of truth: every diagnostic claim must be traceable to Phase 2 metrics or the source document. Do not invent findings, scores, or behaviors.
  • No weasel words: do not use 'consider', 'suggest', 'might', 'could potentially', 'perhaps', 'it may be worth'. Use active verbs: Implement, Eliminate, Establish, Automate, Enforce.
  • Every actionable recommendation MUST cite at least one tactic ID from the VERIFIED_TACTICS_DATABASE in the form [TAC-XXX-NNN]. IDs are copied verbatim — do not invent or paraphrase them.
  • No fabricated numbers: any percentage in prose must correspond to a value present in Phase 2 metrics. No invented dollar amounts, headcounts, or account numbers.
  • The three persona summaries must AGREE on facts; they differ only in lens, vocabulary, and emphasis.
  • Forensic tone, not consultative: describe findings and prescribed actions; do not offer opinions or editorialize.
03Dual-Stream Scoring Model

Two Streams. One Diagnosis.

Every criterion in the knowledge base has two opposing streams, just as the current Engine contrasts Adaptability vs. Taylorism. For FinOps:

Stream A

FinOps Maturity

Detects: Evidence of best practices, automation, organizational capability.

Direction: Higher = more mature.

Stream B

Cloud Cost Anti-Patterns

Detects: Evidence of dysfunction, waste, organisational friction.

Direction: Higher = more problems.

Combined Readings — The Full Story

  • High Maturity + Low Anti-Patterns Genuinely mature (Run)
  • High Maturity + High Anti-Patterns Aspirational but inconsistent (Walk with friction)
  • Low Maturity + High Anti-Patterns Significant transformation needed (Crawl)
  • Low Maturity + Low Anti-Patterns Early stage — not yet enough activity for either signal
04Batch Definitions

The 50-Criteria FinOps Knowledge Base

Five batches. Each batch holds 5 maturity indicators (Stream A) paired against 5 anti-patterns (Stream B). Each criterion carries 3 sub-criteria — the traceable evidence checks the auditor must pass.

A
Cost Visibility & Allocation
5 maturity indicators · 5 anti-patterns · 30 sub-criteria
Stream A · Maturity Indicators
A1
Comprehensive Cost Allocation & Tagging
A consistent, enforced tagging and labeling strategy across all cloud resources. Tags must map spend to business units, applications, environments, and cost centers. Untagged resources must be actively tracked and remediated.
  1. Is there a documented and enforced tagging policy that maps cloud resources to business units, applications, and environments?
  2. Are untagged or mis-tagged resources actively tracked, reported, and remediated within a defined SLA?
  3. Does the tagging strategy support both showback/chargeback and cost optimization workflows?
A2
Showback & Chargeback Models
Mechanisms to attribute cloud costs back to consuming teams or business units — showback (visibility without billing) or full chargeback (actual cost transfer). The model must be transparent and accepted by stakeholders.
  1. Is there a functioning showback or chargeback model that attributes cloud costs to specific teams, products, or business units?
  2. Do consuming teams accept and understand their cost allocations without disputing the methodology?
  3. Is the allocation model refined regularly to reflect changes in shared service usage and architecture?
A3
Cost Anomaly Detection & Alerting
Automated systems to detect unexpected cost spikes, billing anomalies, and budget threshold breaches. Alerts must reach the right stakeholders in near-real-time with actionable context.
  1. Are automated anomaly detection systems in place that identify unexpected cost spikes within 24 hours?
  2. Do alerts include actionable context (affected service, account, magnitude) and route to the responsible team?
  3. Is there a defined response process for cost anomalies with documented escalation paths?
A4
Cloud Cost Dashboards & Reporting
Accessible, role-appropriate cost dashboards for all stakeholders. Engineering sees resource-level detail; finance sees business-unit rollups; executives see trends and forecasts. Dashboards must be current, not stale.
  1. Are cloud cost dashboards available to all relevant stakeholders (engineering, finance, executives) with role-appropriate views?
  2. Is cost data refreshed at least daily and presented with trend analysis and forecast projections?
  3. Do dashboards integrate data from all cloud providers and SaaS platforms into a unified view?
A5
Unit Economics & Cost-Per-Transaction
Cloud cost efficiency measured in business terms — cost per customer, per transaction, per API call, per revenue dollar. Raw spend is insufficient; unit economics connect cloud investment to business value.
  1. Does the organization track unit economics (cost per customer, per transaction, per API call) as a primary efficiency metric?
  2. Are unit cost trends monitored over time to separate growth-driven spend increases from efficiency degradation?
  3. Are engineering teams given unit cost targets and held accountable for cost efficiency alongside performance SLAs?
Stream B · Anti-Patterns
A1-T
Tag Sprawl & Missing Tags
Cloud resources lack consistent tagging or have inconsistent, redundant tag taxonomies. Cost allocation is impossible or unreliable. Untagged spend is treated as acceptable overhead rather than a governance failure.
  1. Is a significant percentage (>20%) of cloud spend untagged or unallocable to a specific team or application?
  2. Is there no enforced tagging standard, with teams creating ad-hoc tags that differ across accounts and providers?
  3. Is untagged spend treated as acceptable 'shared infrastructure' rather than a governance failure requiring remediation?
A2-T
Black Box Cloud Spend
Cloud costs are visible only to a small group (typically finance or a single cloud admin). Engineering teams cannot see the cost of the resources they provision. Cost is discovered only during monthly invoice shock.
  1. Is cloud cost data restricted to finance or a single admin, invisible to the engineering teams who generate the spend?
  2. Do teams discover cost overruns only through monthly invoice reviews rather than real-time visibility?
  3. Is there no self-service cost dashboard available to engineering teams for their own services?
A3-T
Delayed Cost Reporting
Cost data is stale — reports arrive days or weeks after the spend occurs. By the time anomalies are discovered, the damage is done. No near-real-time cost signal exists.
  1. Is cost reporting delayed by more than 48 hours, preventing timely response to anomalies?
  2. Are cost reports generated manually (spreadsheets, monthly exports) rather than through automated pipelines?
  3. Is there no automated alerting for cost anomalies, relying instead on human discovery during periodic reviews?
A4-T
Siloed Cost Views
Each cloud provider, account, or team has its own cost view with no unified perspective. Total cloud economics is unknown. Finance sees invoices; engineering sees resource metrics; nobody sees both together.
  1. Does each cloud provider, account, or team have its own isolated cost view with no cross-cutting unified dashboard?
  2. Is it impossible to answer 'What is our total cost per customer?' because data is fragmented across systems?
  3. Do finance and engineering use different tools and numbers to discuss the same cloud spend?
A5-T
Vanity Cost Metrics
The organization tracks meaningless or misleading cost metrics — total spend without context, percentage discounts without baseline, or savings numbers that cannot be verified. Metrics look good on slides but drive no real optimization.
  1. Does the organization report cost metrics that lack business context (e.g., 'we saved 20%' without baseline or verification)?
  2. Are FinOps dashboards designed for executive presentation rather than operational decision-making?
  3. Is there no connection between reported cost metrics and actual engineering behavior change?
B
Rate & Usage Optimization
5 maturity indicators · 5 anti-patterns · 30 sub-criteria
Stream A · Maturity Indicators
B1
Commitment-Based Discounts (RIs, Savings Plans, CUDs)
Active management of commitment-based discount instruments (Reserved Instances, Savings Plans, Committed Use Discounts) to reduce on-demand pricing. Coverage targets must be set, tracked, and optimized.
  1. Does the organization actively purchase and manage commitment-based discounts (RIs, Savings Plans, CUDs) with defined coverage targets?
  2. Is commitment utilization tracked and optimized, with unused commitments identified and exchanged or resold?
  3. Is there a regular review cadence (at least quarterly) to adjust commitment portfolio based on usage evolution?
B2
Right-Sizing & Workload Optimization
Continuous utilization analysis with right-sizing of instances, databases, and services to match actual demand. Over-provisioned resources represent waste; under-provisioned resources create performance risk.
  1. Is there an ongoing right-sizing program that analyzes CPU, memory, and storage utilization across compute resources?
  2. Are right-sizing recommendations generated automatically and reviewed by engineering teams on a regular cadence?
  3. Does the organization track the financial impact of right-sizing actions and report savings achieved?
B3
Waste Elimination (Orphaned Resources & Idle Instances)
Active identification and termination of orphaned resources (unattached volumes, unused IPs, idle load balancers), zombie instances, and dev/test environments left running outside business hours.
  1. Are orphaned and idle resources (unattached volumes, unused IPs, idle instances) systematically identified and terminated?
  2. Is there an automated process to shut down non-production environments outside business hours or defined schedules?
  3. Does the organization measure and report waste as a percentage of total spend with reduction targets?
B4
Spot & Preemptible Instance Strategy
Spot/preemptible instances leveraged for fault-tolerant and batch workloads to achieve significant rate reductions. Workload architecture must support interruption handling.
  1. Does the organization use spot/preemptible instances for fault-tolerant workloads (batch processing, CI/CD, testing)?
  2. Is there an architectural pattern for graceful handling of spot interruptions with automatic fallback?
  3. Is spot instance savings tracked as a distinct optimization category with coverage expansion targets?
B5
Storage Lifecycle & Data Tiering
Storage lifecycle policies that automatically tier data from hot to warm to cold storage based on access patterns. Retention policies enforced to prevent unbounded growth.
  1. Are storage lifecycle policies implemented to automatically tier data based on access patterns (hot/warm/cold/archive)?
  2. Are data retention policies defined and enforced to prevent unbounded storage growth?
  3. Does the organization monitor storage cost per GB and optimize for the appropriate storage class?
Stream B · Anti-Patterns
B1-T
Commitment Avoidance
The organization runs predominantly on on-demand pricing despite stable, predictable workloads. Fear of commitment results in paying 40–70% premiums unnecessarily.
  1. Does the organization run >50% of stable workloads on on-demand pricing despite predictable usage patterns?
  2. Is commitment avoidance driven by fear of lock-in or forecasting uncertainty rather than rational analysis?
  3. Has the organization never calculated the on-demand premium it pays compared to commitment-based alternatives?
B2-T
Chronic Over-Provisioning
Resources systematically oversized "just in case." Instances run at 5–15% CPU utilization. Databases provisioned for peak that never arrives. Nobody right-sizes because nobody owns the waste.
  1. Are compute resources systematically oversized with average utilization below 20%?
  2. Is over-provisioning justified by 'peak capacity' requirements that are never validated against actual usage data?
  3. Are right-sizing recommendations ignored or backlogged because no team owns the responsibility to act on them?
B3-T
Resource Hoarding & Zombie Resources
Teams accumulate cloud resources they no longer use — stopped instances with attached storage, orphaned snapshots, unused IPs, dev environments left running permanently. Cleanup is nobody's job.
  1. Are there significant numbers of stopped instances, orphaned volumes, unused snapshots, or idle load balancers accruing charges?
  2. Do development and test environments run 24/7 despite being used only during business hours?
  3. Is there no automated lifecycle management or scheduled cleanup of non-production resources?
B4-T
Manual-Only Optimization
Cost optimization is a manual, periodic exercise — someone reviews a spreadsheet quarterly and makes recommendations. There is no automation, no continuous optimization, no integration with engineering workflows.
  1. Is cost optimization performed manually through periodic reviews rather than continuous automated processes?
  2. Are optimization recommendations delivered as reports or tickets that sit in backlogs rather than automated actions?
  3. Is there no integration between cost optimization tooling and engineering CI/CD or deployment workflows?
B5-T
Discount Stacking Ignorance
The organization does not understand how discounts interact across providers. Savings Plans applied to already-discounted workloads, conflicting commitment types, or unused negotiated rates.
  1. Does the organization lack understanding of how discount mechanisms interact (RIs vs Savings Plans vs EDPs)?
  2. Are commitments purchased without analysis of overlap, leaving some workloads double-covered and others uncovered?
  3. Is there no systematic tracking of discount utilization rates across commitment types?
C
Governance & Policy
5 maturity indicators · 5 anti-patterns · 30 sub-criteria
Stream A · Maturity Indicators
C1
Cloud Financial Policy Framework
A documented cloud financial management policy framework covering spend limits, approval workflows, resource provisioning standards, and accountability structures. Policies must be enforced, not aspirational.
  1. Is there a documented cloud financial policy framework covering spend limits, provisioning standards, and approval workflows?
  2. Are policies actively enforced through automated guardrails (e.g., Service Control Policies, Azure Policy, OPA) rather than manual review?
  3. Is there a regular policy review cadence to update rules based on new services, pricing changes, and organizational shifts?
C2
Budget Management & Forecasting
Cloud budgets at team, project, and organizational levels with accurate forecasting. Budget-to-actual variance tracked monthly, with variance analysis driving corrective action.
  1. Are cloud budgets maintained at team, project, and organizational levels with monthly budget-to-actual tracking?
  2. Does the forecasting model incorporate historical trends, planned capacity changes, and business growth projections?
  3. Is budget variance analysis performed monthly with documented corrective actions for overruns?
C3
FinOps Operating Model (Team, Roles, RACI)
A defined FinOps operating model with clear roles, responsibilities, and RACI matrices. Whether centralized, federated, or hybrid, the model must have executive sponsorship and operational cadence.
  1. Is there a defined FinOps operating model with explicit roles (FinOps Practitioner, Cloud Economist, Engineering Lead) and RACI matrix?
  2. Does the operating model have executive sponsorship with regular steering committee engagement?
  3. Is there a documented operational cadence (daily, weekly, monthly) for FinOps reviews and optimization cycles?
C4
Procurement & Vendor Management
A structured approach to cloud procurement including Enterprise Discount Programs (EDPs), negotiated pricing, and multi-year agreements. Vendor relationships are actively managed, not passively consumed.
  1. Does the organization leverage Enterprise Discount Programs, private pricing, or negotiated rates with cloud providers?
  2. Is there a structured vendor management process that evaluates cloud provider pricing against market benchmarks?
  3. Are multi-year commitment decisions made with documented business justification and exit strategy considerations?
C5
Compliance & Regulatory Cost Controls
Cloud financial operations comply with regulatory requirements including data residency cost implications, audit trail requirements, and financial reporting standards.
  1. Are data residency and sovereignty requirements factored into cloud architecture decisions with cost impact analysis?
  2. Does the organization maintain audit trails for cloud financial decisions that satisfy regulatory requirements?
  3. Are compliance-driven cost increases (e.g., dedicated tenancy, encryption, logging) tracked and justified separately?
Stream B · Anti-Patterns
C1-T
Shadow IT & Unmanaged Cloud Accounts
Teams or individuals create cloud accounts outside the governed organizational structure. Spend occurs on personal cards, departmental accounts, or unlinked accounts. Total cloud exposure is unknown.
  1. Do teams or individuals provision cloud resources outside the governed organizational account structure?
  2. Is there spend on personal credit cards, departmental accounts, or unlinked cloud accounts that escapes central visibility?
  3. Is the total number of cloud accounts and total organizational cloud exposure unknown?
C2-T
Budget Blowout Tolerance
Cloud budgets are set but not enforced. Overruns are discovered after the fact with no consequences. Forecasts are fiction. The budget process is a compliance exercise disconnected from operational reality.
  1. Are cloud budget overruns routinely tolerated with no consequences or corrective action required?
  2. Are forecasts consistently inaccurate (>20% variance) without investigation into root causes?
  3. Is the cloud budgeting process a compliance exercise disconnected from operational decision-making?
C3-T
FinOps Theater (Process Without Teeth)
The organization has FinOps titles, meetings, and dashboards but no actual optimization outcomes. FinOps is a checkbox for management reporting, not an operational discipline.
  1. Does the organization have FinOps roles, meetings, or dashboards that produce no measurable optimization outcomes?
  2. Are FinOps activities focused on reporting to management rather than driving engineering behavior change?
  3. Is FinOps maturity self-assessed as 'Walk' or 'Run' without evidence of actual optimization savings?
C4-T
Vendor Lock-In Blindness
Major cloud commitments (multi-year EDPs, proprietary service adoption) are made without evaluating exit costs, portability, or competitive alternatives. Lock-in is accepted by default rather than managed deliberately.
  1. Are major cloud commitments (EDPs, proprietary services) made without exit cost or portability analysis?
  2. Is vendor lock-in accepted by default rather than managed as a deliberate, risk-assessed strategy?
  3. Does the organization lack competitive cost benchmarking against alternative providers or architectures?
C5-T
Compliance as Cost Afterthought
Regulatory and compliance requirements create cost surprises because they are not integrated into architecture planning. Data residency, encryption mandates, and audit requirements add unplanned spend.
  1. Do compliance requirements (data residency, encryption, logging) create unplanned cost surprises?
  2. Is regulatory cost impact not considered during architecture design reviews?
  3. Are compliance-driven infrastructure choices (dedicated tenancy, specific regions) made without cost impact analysis?
D
Architecture & Engineering
5 maturity indicators · 5 anti-patterns · 30 sub-criteria
Stream A · Maturity Indicators
D1
Cost-Aware Architecture Design
Engineering teams treat cost as a first-class architecture constraint alongside performance, reliability, and security. Architecture reviews include cost modeling. The cheapest architecture that meets requirements wins.
  1. Is cost explicitly included as a first-class constraint in architecture design reviews alongside performance and reliability?
  2. Are architecture proposals required to include cost modeling (estimated monthly spend, scaling cost curves)?
  3. Do engineering teams have access to cost data during development and can see the cost impact of their design choices?
D2
Infrastructure as Code with Cost Guardrails
Infrastructure provisioning is codified (Terraform, Pulumi, CloudFormation) with embedded cost guardrails. Policy-as-code prevents oversized or non-compliant resources before deployment.
  1. Is all cloud infrastructure provisioned through Infrastructure as Code (Terraform, Pulumi, CloudFormation)?
  2. Are cost guardrails embedded in the IaC pipeline (e.g., Infracost, OPA policies) to prevent over-provisioning before deployment?
  3. Is there a cost estimation step in the CI/CD pipeline that shows predicted cost impact of infrastructure changes?
D3
Automated Scaling Policies
Automated horizontal and vertical scaling based on demand signals to avoid both over-provisioning (waste) and under-provisioning (performance degradation). Scaling policies must be tested and monitored.
  1. Are automated horizontal and vertical scaling policies implemented for production workloads based on demand signals?
  2. Are scaling policies tested (including scale-to-zero) to ensure they respond correctly to traffic patterns?
  3. Is the cost impact of scaling events monitored to identify over-aggressive or under-responsive scaling configurations?
D4
Multi-Cloud & Hybrid Cost Management
For organizations operating across multiple cloud providers or hybrid environments: unified cost visibility, consistent tagging, and cross-provider optimization. A single-pane-of-glass for total cloud economics.
  1. Does the organization have unified cost visibility across all cloud providers and hybrid environments in a single view?
  2. Is tagging and cost allocation consistent across providers, enabling apples-to-apples comparison?
  3. Are workload placement decisions informed by cross-provider cost analysis (not just technical preference)?
D5
Containerization & Serverless Optimization
Container orchestration (Kubernetes) optimized for cost — pod right-sizing, cluster autoscaling, bin-packing. Serverless workloads monitored for execution cost and duration optimization.
  1. Are Kubernetes clusters optimized for cost (pod right-sizing, node autoscaling, bin-packing, spot node pools)?
  2. Are serverless function costs monitored with optimization for execution duration, memory allocation, and concurrency?

0–3, Mapped to Crawl / Walk / Run

The FinOps Foundation's own maturity model uses Crawl / Walk / Run. The Engine's 0–3 evidence scale maps directly:

0
Absent
Below Crawl
No evidence in any submitted document. The topic is not mentioned, not documented, not practiced. The silence itself is the finding.
1
Weak
Crawl
Buzzwords, aspirational language, or stated intent. "We plan to implement tagging." "Cost optimization is a priority." Intent without mechanism.
2
Clear

3
Strong

A document saying "we are committed to cost optimization" is Score 1, maximum. Only documented mechanisms with traceable implementation details reach Score 2 or 3. This is the Clean Room Protocol — the AI cannot infer, assume, or give credit for good intentions.

An Orthogonal Dimension — Seven Evidence Categories

The 0–3 scale answers how strong is the evidence? A second, orthogonal dimension answers what kind of evidence is it? Every captured evidence quote in Phase 1 carries exactly one of seven category tags. The report can then answer questions like "do we have process evidence but no automation evidence?" — a common pattern in organizations that have written a policy without operationalizing it.

Policy
Written rules, standards, formal documents that DECLARE intent.
Process
Recurring human practices or workflows described as actually happening.
Operational
Day-to-day tactical activities and roles.
Automation
Code, scripts, or platform features that ENFORCE without human intervention.
Accountability
Mechanisms that assign ownership and consequences (showback, chargeback, KPIs).
Financial-Integration
Cost data wired into financial systems or business decisions.
Cultural
Beliefs, norms, and incentives that shape behavior.

Tagging rules: if a quote could fit multiple categories, the auditor picks the dominant one. Automation supersedes Policy when the quote describes enforcement, not declaration. Cultural supersedes Process when the quote describes a norm, not a scheduled activity. Phase 2 aggregates a per-criterion category footprint rendered as chips alongside each forensic audit card, and a global evidence_category_totals across both streams.

08Phase 2 — Mathematical Validation Model

The Firewall Between AI and Strategy

Phase 2 contains zero AI — only deterministic arithmetic applied to Phase 1's raw scores.

Inputs from Phase 1

  • 25 maturity criteria scores (0–3 each, max possible = 75)
  • 25 anti-pattern criteria scores (0–3 each, max possible = 75)
  • 150 sub-criteria verdicts (met / not met)

Calculated Metrics

FinOps Readiness0–100 (composite)
finops_readiness = ((maturity_sum + (75 - antipattern_sum)) / 150) * 100
Composite of maturity points earned and anti-pattern relief. The headline gauge. Higher = closer to embedded FinOps practice.
Maturity Ratio0–100
maturity_ratio = (criteria scored 3 / 25) * 100
Share of the 25 maturity criteria scored as fully embedded (3 of 3 sub-criteria met).
Maturity Depth0–100
maturity_depth = (sum of all maturity scores / 75) * 100
Average maturity score across all 25 criteria on a 0–3 scale, normalized to 0–100%. Captures partial progress that maturity_ratio misses.
Anti-Pattern Burden0–100
antipattern_burden = (sum of all anti-pattern scores / 75) * 100
Average severity across all 25 anti-patterns. Higher = more friction blocking current FinOps practice.
Crawl / Walk / Run Classificationcategorical
if finops_readiness < 33: "Crawl" elif < 66 and antipattern_burden > 50: "Walk with significant friction" elif < 66: "Walk" else: "Run"
Single-word verdict that drives the headline copy in the dashboard.
Delivery Integrity0–100 %
delivery_integrity = (criteria with valid LLM response / 50) * 100
Did the pipeline finish? Share of 50 criteria the LLM returned valid data for. Below 100% means batches failed and the audit is incomplete.
Evidence Density0–100 %
evidence_density = (criteria with ≥1 evidence quote / 50) * 100
Did the source actually say anything? Share of 50 criteria where the audit captured at least one quotable excerpt. Distinct from delivery_integrity — separates "LLM finished" from "source had content."
Domain Breakdownper batch A–E
category_scores[batch] = sum of 5 maturity scores in that batch (0–15)
Drives the radar chart. Enables claims like "Your Governance is Walk-level but your Optimization is still Crawl."
Evidence Category Footprint7 categories
evidence_category_totals = aggregate count of quotes per category across both streams
Orthogonal to score. Answers "what kind of evidence dominates?" — and equally, "what kind is missing?"
Derived Findingsdiagnostic outputs

Key Blockers: Anti-pattern items scored 3 (strong dysfunction evidence).

Critical Gaps: Maturity items scored 0 (complete absence of practice).

Silent Areas: Items where both streams scored 0 — topic not covered by documents at all.

What Makes Phase 3 Trustworthy

Phase 3 cannot fabricate optimism or hide problems because:

10Post-Phase-3 Verification

What Happens After the Strategy Is Drafted

Phase 3 produces a candidate strategy. Four independent verification layers run before the candidate becomes the artifact the user sees. Each layer can downgrade the Quality Gate decision; each layer leaves a visible trace in the report.

Fact-Check Passindependent LLM
A second model call reads the three persona summaries and the roadmap, extracts every concrete factual claim (percentages, named tools/companies, organizational assertions), and classifies each as supported_by_source, supported_by_audit, or unsupported. For unsupported claims it also emits a failure_type (fabricated_number / unverifiable_entity / unsupported_org_claim / out_of_scope / other), a source_location (which persona summary or the roadmap), and a missing_material hint (what source evidence would have made the claim supportable).
Regenerate Loopup to 2 retries
If any claims are unsupported, the strategy is regenerated. The correction appendix is grouped by failure mode with type-specific guidance — the model is told why a claim failed, not just that it failed. "You invented a number. Do not replace it with another invented number; reference the metric generically or omit." Up to two retries. After that, surviving unsupported claims pass through to the output — they are never silently kept.
Phase 3 Grounding Validatordeterministic
Deterministic post-LLM checks. Every [TAC-XXX-NNN] citation is verified against the tactics database — unknown IDs fail. Forbidden weasel phrases scanned across all three summaries and roadmap actions. Percentages cross-referenced against Phase 2 metrics. Driven by finops_validation_rules.json as the source of truth — editing the JSON changes engine behavior.
Quality Gate AggregationGO / WARN / BLOCK
A final deterministic aggregator reads evidence density, Phase 1 completeness, Phase 3 grounding errors, and fact-check survivors. Returns an explicit single-word decision with named blocking reasons or warnings. Surfaced as a coloured banner above the report and embedded in the downloaded HTML — the artifact ships with its own confidence label.
Confidence Notesinline, per persona
When unsupported claims survive both retries, each affected persona summary in the dashboard and report carries a labeled callout listing the unverified statements found in that summary, with the rationale and failure type. Readers see them adjacent to the prose — not buried in a status banner. The product pitch is Traceable Evidence; the artifact admits its own failures.
Source Coverage Gapsactionable feedback
For each surviving unsupported claim, the missing_material hints are grouped by failure type and rendered as an actionable panel — "To strengthen the next assessment, include: a tagging policy document, monthly cost review meeting notes, named FinOps team headcount…". Turns post-hoc verification failures into a collection roadmap for the user's next submission cycle.
Drift Detection Suiteper-pack regression test
Nine bundled golden fixtures: three maturity-stratified (Crawl / Walk / Run) and six Tier 1 document-type fixtures (governance policy, tagging policy, CoE charter, cloud strategy, RI/SP playbook, optimization review). Every layer above is independently visible in the artifact.
